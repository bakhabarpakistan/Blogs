Pakistan Must Reconsider Babar Azam & Rizwan

The ongoing Asia Cup has reignited the debate about Pakistan’s team selection and its future strategy in T20 cricket. While India have showcased a lineup full of attacking players, strong spinners, and fast bowlers with discipline, Pakistan’s experimental approach has exposed shortcomings that cannot be ignored.

India’s Example: Talent and Discipline

India entered the Asia Cup without stalwarts like Virat Kohli and Rohit Sharma, yet their young guns such as Shubman Gill, Yashasvi Jaiswal, Tilak Varma, and Ruturaj Gaikwad stepped up. They not only maintained high strike rates but also proved themselves against strong bowling attacks from Sri Lanka and Bangladesh. Their power hitters like Rinku Singh and Sanju Samson have consistently delivered under pressure. This is not luck—it’s the result of disciplined training, merit-based selection, and trust in long-term performers.

Pakistan’s Current Struggles

In contrast, Pakistan’s young players such as Saim Ayub, Fakhar Zaman, and Azam Khan have only shown flashes of brilliance against weaker sides like Nepal or Afghanistan. Against stronger opposition, their inconsistency becomes obvious. The team’s batting collapses highlight the lack of experience and inability to rotate strike under pressure.

The Captaincy Dilemma

The current captain has been widely questioned for his T20 record. His stats do not reflect the leadership or batting performance required at the highest level. Captaincy in T20 cricket is not just about making field changes—it requires tactical acumen, quick decision-making, and the ability to lead by example. Unfortunately, these qualities have been missing.

Why Babar Azam and Rizwan Are Still Vital

Dropping Babar Azam and Mohammad Rizwan at the same time has backfired. While critics argue about their strike rates, their experience and ability to anchor innings cannot be dismissed. Both players have proven themselves in ICC tournaments and against world-class oppositions. Unlike the new faces, they have delivered in crunch matches:

  • Babar’s consistent runs against India and Australia.
  • Rizwan’s match-winning innings in the 2021 T20 World Cup against India.
  • Their combined partnerships which often provided stability in otherwise fragile batting orders.

Learning from India

India did not discard Kohli and Sharma. Even in transitional phases, they kept their senior players around to groom the youngsters. Kohli’s experience was crucial in India’s recent T20 World Cup win, while Sharma’s leadership brought balance to the squad. Pakistan, on the other hand, has prematurely sidelined its backbone without preparing proper replacements.

The Way Forward

Pakistan must blend experience with youth. Relying solely on untested youngsters in high-stakes tournaments is a gamble that rarely pays off. The Pakistan Cricket Board (PCB) must reconsider its approach:

  • Reinstate Babar Azam and Mohammad Rizwan in the T20 setup.
  • Focus on merit-based selection rather than experiments.
  • Develop young talent gradually, allowing them to learn alongside seniors.
  • Improve fitness standards and adopt the discipline seen in India’s setup.

Conclusion

Pakistan cricket is at a crossroads. Ignoring proven performers like Babar and Rizwan while depending entirely on inexperienced players is a mistake. If Pakistan wants to compete with teams like India, they must combine talent, experience, and discipline. The Asia Cup should serve as a wake-up call: it’s time to bring back merit, respect seniority, and build a balanced squad capable of challenging the best in the world.
